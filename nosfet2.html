1. Semiconductor Basics Required for MOSFET

Explanation: MOSFETs are built on semiconductor substrates. Understanding carrier concentration, Fermi level, and doping is essential. In intrinsic semiconductors, electron and hole concentrations are equal (n = p = ni). Doping creates n-type (donors, ND) or p-type (acceptors, NA) materials.

Carrier Concentration Derivation:

For n-type semiconductor at thermal equilibrium:

n0 ≈ ND, p0 = ni2/ND

For p-type semiconductor:

p0 ≈ NA, n0 = ni2/NA

where ni = intrinsic carrier concentration = √(NCNV)e-Eg/2kT

Physical Intuition: Think of doping as adding "seats" for electrons (n-type) or "empty seats" for holes (p-type). The Fermi level shifts toward the conduction band in n-type and toward the valence band in p-type materials.
Common Mistake: Confusing majority and minority carriers. In n-type, electrons are majority carriers; holes are minority. Many students incorrectly assume both carriers contribute equally to conduction in doped semiconductors.

Practice Problems:

Calculate minority carrier concentration

A silicon sample is doped with 1016 cm-3 phosphorus atoms. Given ni = 1.5×1010 cm-3 at 300K, find the hole concentration.

Solution: n0 ≈ ND = 1016 cm-3. Using mass action law: p0 = ni2/n0 = (1.5×1010)2/1016 = 2.25×104 cm-3
Fermi level shift calculation

Find the shift in Fermi level for silicon doped with 1017 cm-3 boron atoms at 300K. Assume EF - Ev = kT ln(NV/NA) and NV = 1.04×1019 cm-3.

Solution: EF - Ev = (0.0259) ln(1.04×1019/1017) = 0.0259 × ln(104) ≈ 0.0259 × 4.64 ≈ 0.12 eV. The Fermi level is 0.12 eV above the valence band.
Temperature dependence

If temperature increases from 300K to 400K, how does intrinsic carrier concentration change? Given Eg = 1.12 eV for Si.

Solution: ni ∝ T3/2e-Eg/2kT. Ratio = (400/300)3/2 × e[-1.12/(2×8.617×10-5)](1/400 - 1/300) ≈ 1.54 × e[-6500](-8.33×10-4) ≈ 1.54 × e5.41 ≈ 1.54 × 224 ≈ 345. So ni increases by ~345×.

2. MOS Capacitor Physics

Explanation: The MOS capacitor forms the heart of MOSFET operation. It consists of Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor layers. Applying gate voltage creates electric field that modulates surface potential.

Threshold Voltage Derivation:

At threshold, surface potential ψs = 2φF, where φF = (kT/q)ln(NA/ni) for p-substrate

Depletion charge: Qdep = -qNAWdm = -√(2qεsiNA·2φF)

Flat-band voltage: VFB = φms - Qox/Cox

VTH = VFB + 2φF + γ√(2φF)

where γ = √(2qεsiNA)/Cox is body effect coefficient

Physical Intuition: Imagine the oxide as a capacitor dielectric. When gate voltage exceeds threshold, it "pulls" enough electrons to the surface to form an inversion layer (channel). Before that, it just depletes majority carriers.
Common Mistake: Forgetting that threshold voltage depends on substrate doping. Higher NA increases |VTH| for nMOS. Many students treat VTH as a fixed parameter.

Practice Problems:

Calculate threshold voltage

For an nMOS with p-substrate NA = 1016 cm-3, tox = 10 nm, φms = -0.9 V, Qox = 0. Calculate VTH. Given: εox = 3.9ε0, εsi = 11.7ε0, ni = 1.5×1010 cm-3.

Solution: φF = 0.0259 ln(1016/1.5×1010) ≈ 0.347 V. Cox = εox/tox = (3.9×8.85×10-14)/(10×10-7) ≈ 3.45×10-7 F/cm2. γ = √(2×1.6×10-19×11.7×8.85×10-14×1016)/3.45×10-7 ≈ 0.41 V1/2. VTH = -0.9 + 2×0.347 + 0.41×√(2×0.347) ≈ -0.9 + 0.694 + 0.41×0.833 ≈ 0.135 V
Body effect calculation

If VSB = 2V for the above MOSFET, find new VTH. Use γ = 0.41 V1/2, φF = 0.347 V.

Solution: VTH = VTH0 + γ[√(2φF + VSB) - √(2φF)] = 0.135 + 0.41[√(0.694+2) - √0.694] = 0.135 + 0.41[√2.694 - 0.833] = 0.135 + 0.41[1.641 - 0.833] = 0.135 + 0.41×0.808 ≈ 0.466 V
Flat-band voltage

Calculate VFB for Al gate on p-Si with NA = 1015 cm-3. Given: φm(Al) = 4.1 eV, χ(Si) = 4.05 eV, Eg = 1.12 eV, Qox/q = 1011 cm-2, tox = 20 nm.

Solution: φsi = χ + Eg/2 + φF = 4.05 + 0.56 + 0.0259ln(1015/1.5×1010) ≈ 4.61 + 0.288 = 4.898 eV. φms = φm - φsi = 4.1 - 4.898 = -0.798 V. Cox = 3.9×8.85e-14/(20e-7) = 1.725e-7 F/cm2. Qox/Cox = (1.6e-19×1e11)/1.725e-7 ≈ 0.093 V. VFB = -0.798 - 0.093 = -0.891 V
